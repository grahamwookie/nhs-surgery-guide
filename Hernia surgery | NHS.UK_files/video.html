1. Booking your appointment with the specialist

Your GP might book your first appointment at the hospital for you. If they don’t, you’ll get a letter with a reference number to book online or over the phone.

You can choose which hospital you want to go to. Once booked, you’ll get a confirmation in the post.

Approx 6 weeks

2. At your appointment

The surgeon will explain what treatment options you have, and their benefits and risks. You can decide the best treatment together. You might decide it’s not surgery.

You can ask any questions. This is the time when you decide if you want to go ahead with the treatment.

This appointment usually lasts 15 to 20 minutes. Your surgeon might also book you the date for your operation. If they don’t you can (book the surgery appointment yourself

Approx 2 weeks

3. Confirming your surgery date

After your appointment with the surgeon you’ll get a letter confirming the date of your operation.

You can change the date (the letter will tell you how) but you might have to wait several weeks to get a new one.

Approx 3 weeks

4. Checking you’re fit for surgery

If you’re having a general anaesthetic, you’ll have an appointment with a nurse at the hospital to make sure you’re healthy enough for surgery. This is called a pre-operative assessment or pre-op.

If you’re not healthy enough, the operation may be postponed.

Preparing for your pre-op appointment

Approx 2 weeks

5. Getting ready for surgery

In the pre-op appointment the nurse will tell you how to prepare for the operation

You might have to do things like:

  • change or stop taking your medicine
  • stop eating and drinking from a set time before your surgery

On the morning of your surgery, you should:

  • have a bath or shower
  • remove all jewellery, including any piercings

6. Day of your surgery

When you arrive at the hospital go to the reception desk at your ward. Bring the letter with the confirmation of your surgery date with you.

The day of your surgery

7. Going home and recovery

You can go home when you feel well enough, and can eat and drink. You must be able to walk (if you’re not in a wheelchair) and pee.

If needed you’ll get medication and painkillers to take home. You’ll also get a discharge letter, explaining your treatment. A copy will be sent to your GP.

If you need a sick note, tell the nurse.

You’ll need someone to help you home by car or taxi. If you go home the same day, you’ll need someone to stay with you for at least 24 hours.
